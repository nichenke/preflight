## Overall assessment

The document is structurally sound and generally well-written. The EARS-style
requirement statements (When <trigger>, the system shall <response>) are used
consistently for FRs. Most requirements are atomic and testable. However, there
are quality issues across completeness, precision, and internal consistency.

---

## Issues found

### High severity

1. NFR-001 duplicates latency already captured in FR-001/FR-002/FR-003
   FR-001 through FR-003 each specify "within 30 seconds" as part of the
   functional trigger→response. NFR-001 restates the same bound as a
   non-functional requirement without adding new information (it adds "p99"
   and "under normal operating conditions" but those qualifiers are absent
   from the FRs). This creates a contradiction: the FRs are unconditional
   ("shall deliver within 30 seconds"), but NFR-001 makes the constraint
   conditional on normal operating conditions. One of these must be the
   authoritative specification; having both creates ambiguity for
   implementation and test planning.

   Recommendation: Remove the latency bound from the FRs or add the p99/
   normal-conditions qualifier there too, and let NFR-001 be the single
   source for latency requirements.

2. No acceptance criteria or measurability for NFR-002 (delivery rate)
   "99.9% delivery rate measured over a rolling 30-day window" is stated, but
   the denominator is undefined. 99.9% of what? Attempted deliveries?
   Notifications that entered the system? Notifications that were not
   explicitly cancelled by the user? The measurement methodology is
   unspecified, making this requirement untestable as written.

3. FR-006 is underspecified for retry behavior
   "Exponential backoff" and "exhausting retries" are stated without any
   bounds. There is no maximum retry count, no maximum backoff interval, and
   no total time-to-failure defined. This leaves the implementation free to
   retry for seconds or hours — both would satisfy the stated requirement.
   A retry that spans hours would violate the spirit of the 30-second
   delivery SLA but not the letter of FR-006.

### Medium severity

4. NFR-003 does not define "degradation"
   "Without degradation in delivery latency or delivery rate" references two
   metrics that are defined elsewhere (30 seconds, 99.9%), but the document
   does not say whether NFR-003 requires the full SLA to hold at peak load or
   merely that no additional degradation occurs relative to normal load. This
   matters: if normal load achieves p95 < 5 seconds, does NFR-003 require
   that to hold at 10k/min, or only that p99 stays under 30 seconds?

5. FR-007 (user preferences) lacks scope definition
   "Per-channel preferences, including enabling or disabling individual
   notification types per channel" — "notification types" is not defined
   anywhere in the document. The triggering events (message, task completion,
   system alert) are implicitly the types, but this is never stated. If a
   user disables "task" notifications for email, does FR-002 still apply? The
   relationship between FR-007 and FR-001/FR-002/FR-003 is unspecified.

6. Missing requirement: notification persistence / read state
   The document specifies in-app notification delivery (FR-005) but says
   nothing about whether notifications are stored, for how long, or whether
   read/unread state is tracked. This is a significant omission for an in-app
   channel — clients polling (per assumptions) need something to poll against.

7. Assumption about event publishing is load-bearing and untested
   The third assumption ("Triggering events are published as structured events
   the notification service can consume") is an architectural dependency that
   should be a constraint or a dependency, not an assumption. If this is not
   true, the entire notification system cannot function. Assumptions should be
   things that are plausible and low-risk to assume; this one, if wrong,
   invalidates the design.

### Low severity

8. No versioning policy for preferences (FR-007)
   It is unstated what happens to existing user preferences when new
   notification types are added in future versions. This is an edge case but
   worth noting as a gap for v1 design.

9. "Approved transactional email provider" in Constraints is not actionable
   The constraint names two examples (SendGrid, SES) but provides no
   mechanism for determining what is "approved." This is aspirational rather
   than constraining — a team could use an unapproved provider and argue the
   constraint is subjective.

10. Document version is 0.1.0 but status is Draft
    Minor: the version/status combination is fine, but there is no review or
    approval workflow defined in the metadata. For a requirements document
    being used to drive design, it is unclear who has authority to promote
    this from Draft to an approved state.
